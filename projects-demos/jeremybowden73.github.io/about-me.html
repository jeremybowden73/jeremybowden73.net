A Bit About Me
The Past

In the 1990s I graduated with an Honours degree in Engineering. This led to gainful employment in the engineering sector, working with companies such as GE, Caterpillar, and Dunlop.

I acquired some skills that helped me do my job...

  • Expert 3D CAD/CAE skills
  • Expert in PLM (Product Lifecycle Management; essentially GitHub for engineers and product designers)
  • Great knowledge of Excel
  • Great engineering knowledge, like knowing the endurance stress limit for most steels is roughly 50% of the tensile strength

And some that helped make me more valuable to my colleagues...

  • Presenting and describing my ideas
  • Communicating with peers both informally and in formal reviews
  • Making and implementing contingency plans, when things went a little bit wonky
  • Adapting to established company cultures
  • Helping others to develop skills when I had greater knowledge or experience
  • Developing a good sense of when to relinquish and when to hold the line
The Present


A 'zen' moment? The stars aligning? An epiphany? Call it what you will; the events of 2017 marked the beginning of my current journey into computer programming.


As a result of

  • being made redundant, and
  • a late night conversation with a friend

I started to think seriously about a change of career, from engineering to computer programming. Since childhood I've had a 'background' interest in computers but I'd not really considered it as a career until the friend said it to me straight. We'd discussed which elements of engineering I enjoyed and had an aptitude for. Four words set me on the path that led me to where I am today: "You should do coding".

I got a book from the library and raced through it, learning how to say "Hello World", the difference between a char and a string, and how pointers work in C.

I found myself consumed: by the sublime logic of the code; by the sense of accomplishment of creating something with a logical, specific purpose; by the refreshing, rewarding feeling of learning something new. And it all made sense! Well, maybe 99%.


So that's why today I call myself a Fledgling Programmer.



The Future


Short-term, my goal is to continue learning programming skills and languages by studying at my own pace with online e-learning providers.


But my eventual objective is to take my career into one of the many areas of "computer programming" that appeal to me. It's such a large field that I can't say, at the moment, exactly what I want to do. That's why I think it's important to focus my learning on programming skills, not just programming languages.
